Using Shutter Speed

What is Shutter Speed?

Shutter speed is the amount of time that your camera's shutter remains open to allow light to come in contact with the camera's image sensor. In film however shutter speed is the amount of time the film is exposed to the scene. It completely can change the way your photo looks. Your camera may have a leaf type shutter or a focal-plane shutter. Aperture and shutter speed work hand and hang together to get the best exposure for the photo. Shutter speed is usually measured in fractions of seconds. Depending on your shutter speed you may want to use a tripod.

How to use Shutter Speed

When taking photos using film ISO, Aperture and shutter speed all work together to create a beautiful photo. But Aperture and Shutter speed especially work together. When choosing a shutter speed you want to be very careful. If you choose wrong, your photo can come out complete opposite of what you hoped.

When and how to use a slow shutter speed

  • 1/30, 1/15, 1/8, 1/3 Will blur shots
  • Slower shutter speeds may require a tripod to keep the camera still
  • Allowing more light into the camera in dark scenes
  • You can pan the camera to add motion to the image while using a slow shutter speed
  • Taking pictures of bodies of water
  • Beautiful shots of traffic, with light trails

When and how to use a faster shutters speed

  • 1/2000, 1/1000, 1/500, 1,250 Will allow you to freeze action
  • Faster shutter speeds are great for action shots. The shots are much more clear and sharp when using a faster shutter speed
  • Freeze Movement
  • When you are in a low light situation and your photos are coming out blurry due to hand movement
Materials, What You'll Need!
  • 35mm Camera
  • 35mm Film
  • Something to take photos of
  • Location to get film developed
